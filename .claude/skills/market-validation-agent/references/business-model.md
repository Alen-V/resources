# Business Model & Unit Economics

Lay out how the business will actually make money, distribute the product, and whether the numbers can work. Pricing was upstream; this step turns price into a viable business.

## Revenue model

Pick one primary revenue model and note any secondary ones. Match the choice to the pricing model from the previous step. Common choices:

- **Subscription (SaaS)** — predictable revenue, requires retention focus
- **Transactional** — fee per transaction; sensitive to volume
- **Marketplace take-rate** — requires both supply and demand to be liquid
- **Advertising** — requires massive scale or premium audience
- **Hardware + recurring** — high upfront capital, recurring revenue tail
- **Services + product** — services bridge to a product
- **Licensing / API** — works for technical infrastructure

Document why this revenue model fits the buyer's preference, the cost structure, and the competitive context.

## Channel strategy

How does the buyer find and buy the product? Be specific.

- **Self-serve** — works when the product is simple, low-priced, and the buyer is the user. Requires content + SEO + paid acquisition.
- **Sales-led (inside / field)** — works for higher ACV ($10k+) and committee buying. Requires SDR + AE motion.
- **Product-led growth** — works when individual users can adopt and pull the org in. Requires viral or workflow-based expansion loops.
- **Partner / channel** — works when partners own the customer relationship (resellers, system integrators).
- **Community-led** — works for developer tools and niche prosumer products.
- **Direct-to-consumer** — performance marketing, influencer, retail.

Mismatch between channel and ACV is one of the most common startup failures. A $99/month product cannot afford a $50k/year AE. Note this explicitly if you see it.

## Unit economics — estimate each

Calculate (or estimate, with stated assumptions) at least these four numbers:

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)** — fully loaded sales + marketing cost to acquire one paying customer.
- Look at competitor benchmarks. Public SaaS company CAC payback periods are typically disclosed in S-1s and 10-Ks.
- For early-stage estimates, use industry benchmarks: PLG SaaS CAC = ~$200–2k, sales-led SaaS = $5k–$50k+, DTC consumer = $20–$200.

**LTV (Lifetime Value)** — gross margin × ARPU × customer lifetime (1 / churn rate).
- Use competitor churn rates as a proxy. Public companies disclose net dollar retention; private companies in the space sometimes do too.

**Gross margin** — revenue minus direct cost of delivering the service.
- SaaS healthy zone: 70–85%
- Marketplace healthy zone: 60–80% on take-rate revenue
- Hardware: 20–50%
- Services: 30–60%

**Payback period** — CAC / (monthly gross-margin-adjusted revenue).
- Under 12 months for self-serve is healthy. 12–24 months for sales-led can work. Over 24 months is a red flag.

State assumptions next to each number. Investors and the user need to know which input to revisit.

## LTV : CAC ratio sanity check

Healthy: 3:1 or better
Marginal: 2:1 to 3:1 — possible with strong organic growth
Dangerous: under 2:1 — the business loses money on every customer at scale

## Defensibility / moat

Note which moats apply, if any:
- Network effects (direct, indirect, two-sided)
- Switching costs (data lock-in, workflow integration, training cost)
- Economies of scale
- Brand
- Proprietary data
- Regulatory licenses
- IP / patents

No moat in early stages is normal, but the *path* to a moat should be plausible. Without one, the business gets commoditized.

## Output for this step

A business model section (300–500 words) covering: revenue model with justification, channel strategy with justification, unit economics with stated assumptions (CAC, LTV, gross margin, payback, LTV:CAC), and a moat paragraph. Flag any obvious unit-economics red flags for the risk register in Step 8.
