# Demand Signals

Determine whether the underlying problem is real and whether people are actively trying to solve it. This is the cheapest, most informative validation step — if no one is looking for the solution, market size estimates don't matter.

## Signal types, in rough order of strength

1. **Money already flowing.** People paying competitors (even bad ones) is the strongest signal. Look at competitor revenue, customer counts, and pricing pages. Covered in detail in the competitor step — note traction signals here too.
2. **Active complaint behavior.** People posting on Reddit, X, forums, or review sites complaining about the problem or the current solutions.
3. **Active search behavior.** Google Trends, "people also ask", autocomplete suggestions, Stack Overflow questions, Quora questions.
4. **Workaround behavior.** People building janky solutions themselves — spreadsheets, Zapier chains, custom scripts. This is "compensating behavior" and indicates real pain.
5. **Adjacent product growth.** Adjacent products growing fast suggests the space is hot.
6. **Funding flowing in.** VCs funding startups in the space is a lagging but real signal. Conversely, repeated failed startups in the same space is a critical signal — investigate why.

## Where to look

**Reddit** — search for subreddit names + the problem. Sort by top of all time and by recent. Look for high-upvote threads about the pain. Look at the comments — quote real user language.
Query pattern: `[problem keyword] reddit`, then visit relevant subreddits directly.

**Google Trends** — search for the problem term and competitor names. Compare. Look at multi-year trend (rising, flat, declining) and geographic distribution. Note: Trends is sensitive to query phrasing; try 2–3 variations.

**Product Hunt** — search for the category. Look at upvote counts on recent launches in the space and the comments — both supportive and critical.

**Review sites** — G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, App Store / Play Store reviews. Especially 2–3 star reviews of existing solutions: these tell you exactly what the gap is.
Query pattern: `[competitor name] g2 reviews`, `[competitor name] alternatives`.

**Stack Overflow / GitHub issues** — for developer-facing problems.

**Hacker News** — search.hn.algolia.com — look at story counts and comment depth on related submissions.

**X / Twitter** — search for the problem phrased as a complaint ("I wish there was a way to ___", "why does ___ suck", "looking for an alternative to ___").

**Forums specific to the industry** — for vertical SaaS, professional forums are gold. For consumer ideas, look at niche communities (e.g. r/Frugal for budgeting, BabyCenter for parenting, etc.).

## What to capture

For each strong signal, capture:
- The platform and source URL
- A verbatim quote (under 15 words, one per source) or close paraphrase
- Approximate engagement (upvotes, comments, search volume direction)
- Date — fresh signals beat stale ones

Aim for 8–15 distinct demand signals across at least 4 different platforms before concluding either way.

## Strong negative signals — surface these explicitly

- Google Trends shows clear multi-year decline
- Threads about the problem are old (5+ years) with no recent activity
- Reviews of existing solutions are mostly positive — gap is small
- Multiple competitors have shut down in the past 24 months
- The problem is solved well enough by free / built-in tools (Excel, native OS features, ChatGPT)
- The "complaints" are about price or convenience of an already-solved problem, not the problem itself

## Output for this step

A demand evidence section (250–500 words) summarizing the signals found, with a confidence rating (Strong / Moderate / Weak / Negative). Include 3–6 of the strongest verbatim quotes with source URLs and dates. This section anchors the report's case for market existence.
